Remy~\cite{winstein2013tcp} demonstrated that computer-generated congestion
control algorithms (CCAs) could outperform decades of human design within their
training distribution. However, Remy's decision tree CCAs generalize poorly
to network conditions outside their training range. Despite this being
identified over a decade ago, we are not aware of prior work that compares
alternative representations on Remy's generalization graphs---training
on the same environments, with the same observation and action spaces.

We evaluate two additional policy representations under Remy's
generalization setup. Keeping the observation space fixed, we ask:
\textbf{how do a neural network and a large language model (LLM)-guided
code evolution system compare to Remy's decision trees on
out-of-distribution generalization?}

We compare three representations:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Decision trees} (Remy): A WhiskerTree maps observation bins to
  actions, optimized by evolutionary search over tree structure.
\item \textbf{Neural networks} (PPO): A neural network maps the same observations
  to the same actions, trained by
  proximal policy optimization (PPO) \cite{schulman2017ppo} in Remy's C++ simulator.
\item \textbf{Source code} (AlphaCC): An LLM generates
  source-code policies mapping observations to actions, evolved by
  mutation and evaluated via Remy's C++ simulator.
\end{enumerate}

All three are evaluated in Remy's C++ simulator across
1--1000~Mbps (Figure~\ref{fig:cpp_gen}). Remy~1x peaks near its
training rate (15~Mbps) but degrades sharply at both extremes,
consistent with~\cite{sivaraman2014experimental}. PPO, trained
with 6 independent brains and no architecture search, underperforms
Remy~1x across most of the rate range---we cannot determine whether
this reflects the neural-network representation or insufficient
tuning. AlphaCC's best evolved policy, LLM-translated to C++ for
evaluation, degrades more gradually than Remy~1x at high rates but
does not surpass it on the aggregate mean.


\section{Related Work}

Remy~\cite{winstein2013tcp} pioneered automated CCA design with decision tree optimization. Sivaraman et al.~\cite{sivaraman2014experimental} studied its generalization, finding that trees trained on narrow ranges degrade on wider conditions while broader training ranges trade peak performance for robustness.

Several deep RL approaches followed: PCC~\cite{dong2015pcc} used online
learning for utility maximization; Aurora~\cite{jay2019deep} applied deep RL to congestion control; Yan et al.~\cite{yan2018pantheon} built the Pantheon benchmarking platform for systematic CCA comparison. These demonstrated that neural networks can learn congestion control but
did not systematically compare generalization across representations on the same environments.

CCAC~\cite{arun2021ccac} introduced formal CCA verification using
SMT solvers. CCmatic~\cite{arun2024ccmatic} extended this to automated
synthesis with formal guarantees, but only within a fixed
window-adjustment template.

AlphaEvolve~\cite{alphaevolve2025} demonstrated LLM-guided code
evolution for mathematical optimization. Our approach adapts this
paradigm to congestion control, using simulation-based fitness instead
of mathematical verification.

Copa~\cite{arun2018copa} and BBR~\cite{cardwell2017bbr} represent hand-designed CCAs that encode domain knowledge about delay-based and model-based congestioncontrol, respectively.

\section{Methods}
\label{sec:methods}

\subsection{Simulation Setup and Evaluation}

\paragraph{Simulation setup.}
We use the same dumbbell network topology as in Remy \cite{winstein2013tcp}, consisting of two senders which randomly start and stop flows. Each flow's duration is sampled from an exponential distribution, and during a flow a sender chooses when and how many packets to send. For each set of CCAs we evaluate, this decision of whether to send a packet at a given time is determined by the congestion window and the minimum time interval between sent packets, and these are directly updated by the action chosen by each policy. To compare the generalization of each CCA learning scheme, we fix all network conditions of the simulator except for the link rate, which we vary in both the learning and evaluation process.

\paragraph{Normalized score.}
To compare across link rates, we use a normalized score:
\begin{equation}
S = \log_2\!\left(\frac{\text{throughput}}{\text{capacity}}\right) - \log_2\!\left(\frac{\text{delay}}{\text{min\_rtt}}\right)
\end{equation}
This measures how close a CCA comes to fully utilizing the link (first term)
while keeping delay near the propagation minimum (second term). A score of 0
means perfect utilization with zero queuing delay.

\paragraph{Evaluation protocol.}
All results use Remy's original C++ simulator (\texttt{sender-runner}).
Unless otherwise noted, we evaluate on 99 log-spaced link rates from
0.1--100~packets/ms (1--1000~Mbps), with RTT${}={}$150~ms, 2~senders,
mean on/off duration 5000~ms, and infinite buffers.
For the main comparison, AlphaCC scores are averaged over 10 random
seeds per rate.

\paragraph{Training and evaluation.}
All three methods are evaluated in the same C++ simulator.
Remy and PPO train directly in C++. 

\paragraph{Comparison summary.}
The three methods share the same observation space (4 exponentially
weighted moving averages, or EWMAs), action
space (window increment, multiplier, intersend), and evaluation protocol,
but differ along other axes (Table~\ref{tab:fairness}).

\subsection{PPO: Deep Reinforcement Learning}
\label{sec:ppo}
For our PPO agent, we consider a single neural network that is executed
locally for each sender that ultimately maps local observations to a decision
about when to send the next packet. The parameters of this neural network are
identical for each sender and unchanged throughout a given simulation. The
neural network accepts the same inputs and uses the same discretized action
space for each action dimension as the Remy decision tree.

\subsubsection{Slotting our PPO agent into the Remy codebase}

We implemented a new \texttt{NeuralRat} class in the original Remy codebase as a
subclass of the existing \texttt{Rat} class used by the Remy decision tree,
isolating the decision-making algorithm from the surrounding agent interface and
simulation infrastructure. This subclass inherits the same packet-sending logic,
memory tracking, and simulation interface, and overrides only the method that
maps the current memory state to window increment, window multiple, and
intersend values. Where the base \texttt{Rat} performs a lookup in the whisker
tree to obtain these values, \texttt{NeuralRat} runs a forward pass through the
neural network.

\subsubsection{Network inputs and action selection}

The network takes as input the four-dimensional observation
$\mathbf{x} = (\text{sewma},\, \text{rewma},\, \text{rtt\_ratio},\, \text{slow\_rewma})$
and produces three unnormalized score vectors
$\mathbf{z}^{\text{wi}} \in \mathbb{R}^{257}$,
$\mathbf{z}^{\text{wm}} \in \mathbb{R}^{101}$, and
$\mathbf{z}^{\text{is}} \in \mathbb{R}^{56}$,
one entry per discretized value in the window increment, window multiple, and
intersend action spaces respectively. Each score vector is independently
normalized into a probability distribution via a temperature-scaled softmax:
%
\begin{equation}
  \pi(a^k_i) = \frac{\exp(z^{k}_i / \tau)}{\sum_j \exp(z^{k}_j / \tau)},
  \qquad k \in \{\text{wi},\, \text{wm},\, \text{is}\},
\end{equation}
%
where $\tau$ is a temperature parameter that controls the entropy of each output
distribution.  At each decision point, an action index is sampled independently
from each of the three categorical distributions, selecting a specific
discretized value for the window increment, window multiple, and minimum
intersend.




\subsection{AlphaCC: LLM-Guided Code Evolution}
\label{sec:alphacc}

Our approach adapts LLM-guided code evolution, inspired by
AlphaEvolve~\cite{alphaevolve2025}, to congestion control. We utilized the open-source version OpenEvolve (https://github.com/algorithmicsuperintelligence/openevolve) and wire it agais


The evolution loop
proceeds as follows:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Seed population}: We initialize with hand-designed CCAs
  implemented as source-code policies. For single-point training:
  additive-increase multiplicative-decrease (AIMD), Copa, an
  adaptive hybrid, and a BBR-like rate estimator. For the diverse-seed study:
  constant (minimal), AIMD, Copa, and pacing seeds (\S\ref{sec:diverse_seeds}).
\item \textbf{Mutation}: GPT-5.3-codex receives the parent policy's source
  code, its fitness score, and the top-performing policies in the archive.
  It generates a mutated policy.
\item \textbf{Evaluation}: The candidate is compiled, sanity-checked (it must
  increase the congestion window when no congestion is detected), and evaluated
  on the training condition.
\item \textbf{Selection}: Parents are chosen with 50\% exploitation (highest
  fitness) and 50\% exploration (random archive member).
\end{enumerate}

We run 15 generations with 5 candidates per generation (75 candidates
for single-point training; for multipoint, each candidate is evaluated
on 6 rates).

\subsubsection{Policy Representation}

Each AlphaCC policy maps Remy's 4-EWMA observation to the same
action triple (window increment, multiplier, intersend). Unlike
Remy's stateless tree lookup or PPO's feedforward pass, AlphaCC
policies can maintain persistent state across calls (e.g., phase
variables, bandwidth estimates). The statefulness ablation
(\S\ref{sec:statefulness_ablation}) partially addresses this confound.
For the main comparison (Figure~\ref{fig:cpp_gen}), we
hand-translated the best evolved policy to C++ as a new sender class
in Remy's codebase, preserving the same state variables, thresholds,
and control logic.

\subsubsection{Mechanistic Convergence}
\label{sec:convergence}

All 12 evolved policies, regardless of seed family,
share recurring BDP-scaling and pacing components:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{BDP proxy}: \texttt{bdp = min\_rtt / rec\_ewma},
  approximating the number of packets in flight at the bottleneck rate
  (a proxy for the bandwidth-delay product in packets). Present in 12/12 policies.
\item \textbf{In-flight scaling}: window increments scale with
  $\sqrt{\text{bdp}}$. Present in 12/12 policies.
\item \textbf{Rate-based pacing}: \texttt{intersend} set from
  receive-rate estimates, not purely window-driven. Present in 12/12
  policies.
\item \textbf{Queue pressure sensing}: RTT ratio thresholds
  (${\sim}$1.3 and ${\sim}$2.0) gate transitions between growth,
  maintenance, and backoff. Present in 12/12 policies.
\item \textbf{Phase machines}: explicit startup/drain/steady state
  transitions, analogous to BBR's state machine. Present in 1/12
  systematic runs (pac\_r1).
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Mechanism coding methodology.}
We inspected each of the 12 final policies and classified the
presence of five structural elements:
\begin{enumerate}
\item BDP estimation: computing ratio of minimum RTT
  to smoothed receive rate, or equivalent;
\item In-flight scaling: gain proportional to
  $\sqrt{\mathrm{bdp}}$ or similar;
\item Rate-based pacing: \texttt{intersend} set from receive-rate;
\item Queue pressure: RTT-ratio thresholds gating
  growth\slash backoff;
\item Phase machines: explicit state variables for startup,
  drain, and steady states.
\end{enumerate}
Coding was performed by one author post hoc; labels were not used
during evolution.

The four seed families encode different CCA approaches: the
constant seed provides \emph{no} congestion-control structure,
AIMD provides loss-based structure, Copa provides delay-based structure,
and pacing provides rate-based structure. All four converge on
in-flight-scaled pacing---including the constant seed, which starts with
zero congestion-control logic. We cannot distinguish whether this
convergence reflects a genuine fitness-landscape attractor or the LLM's
inability to explore beyond familiar TCP templates.

Figure~\ref{fig:mechanism} shows core code excerpted from three
policies evolved from different seed families. Despite different variable
names, smoothing constants, and threshold values, the structural pattern
is shared: estimate BDP from \texttt{min\_rtt / rec\_ewma}, scale
window changes by $\sqrt{\text{BDP}}$, and pace sends at the
estimated receive rate.

\subsubsection{Failure Modes}
\label{sec:failures}

The weaker systematic runs (aimd\_r1 at $-$3.41, constant\_r3 at $-$3.33)
and prior exploratory failures suggest recurring
failure modes:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Architectural lock-in}: The seed plus first 3--5 mutations
  determine the policy's architecture (PID controller vs state machine,
  scale cap, coefficient ranges). Subsequent mutations can only tune
  parameters within that architecture---we did not observe escapes to
  qualitatively different designs.
\item \textbf{Conservative BDP caps}: One failed run committed to
  \texttt{min(16, sqrt(bdp))} early. At high BDP ($>$256 packets), the cap
  makes the policy 6$\times$ slower to grow than the winning policies.
\item \textbf{Over-engineering}: Another failed run built a PID-style
  controller with 5+ interacting error terms, suggesting too many
  coefficients for 15 generations to co-tune.
\item \textbf{Degenerate mutations}: One earlier exploratory run
  (30 generations) produced mutations
  scoring $-$105. We did not observe recovery from
  degenerate regions in any run.
\end{enumerate}

These failure modes are consistent with local-search-like
behavior---effective within an architecture, but in these runs unable to
escape when the initial architecture is poorly suited.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{What the Comparison Shows}

\textbf{Representation shapes generalization profile.} Each
representation degrades differently outside its training distribution.
Remy~1x peaks near training (15~Mbps) and degrades sharply at
both extremes, replicating the behavior found in ~\cite{sivaraman2014experimental}.
AlphaCC's in-flight scaling adapts window increments to
link capacity, producing more gradual degradation at high rates but
losing near training where Remy's precise tree fit dominates.

\textbf{No method dominates everywhere.} Each representation has
a region where it performs best (Figure~\ref{fig:cpp_gen}).

\textbf{PPO.} PPO underperforms Remy~1x across most of the rate range
in the C++ simulator despite sharing the same observations,
actions, and training simulator. We did not conduct a thorough
hyperparameter sweep (6~brains, no architecture search), so we cannot
determine whether this reflects a limitation of the
neural-network representation or insufficient training.

\textbf{Mechanistic convergence.} All 12~AlphaCC runs converge on
in-flight-scaled pacing regardless of seed family
(\S\ref{sec:convergence}). Distinguishing whether this reflects the
LLM's TCP prior or a genuine fitness-landscape attractor would
require a non-TCP-pretrained LLM.

\subsection{Interpretability}

The source-code representation exposes named variables and explicit
control flow. The mechanism analysis in
\S\ref{sec:convergence} relies on this; extracting comparable structure
from PPO's weight matrices would require separate
interpretability work.

\subsection{Limitations}

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{PPO baseline quality}: PPO's performance may partly
  reflect its training setup; further hyperparameter tuning could change
  its relative standing.
\item \textbf{Evolution environment}: AlphaCC policies are evolved
  as source code and hand-translated to C++ for evaluation. Translation
  errors could affect results, though 38/38 unit tests passed.
\item \textbf{LLM prior vs.\ discovery}: The 12/12 convergence on BDP scaling
  could reflect either (a)~rediscovery of a genuine attractor in the fitness
  landscape or (b)~the LLM's inability to explore beyond familiar TCP
  templates. We cannot distinguish these from simulation alone.
\item \textbf{Small N}: 12 runs (4 families $\times$ 3) provides
  initial evidence for convergence but is insufficient for robust statistical
  claims. Bootstrap CIs and more seeds would strengthen the result.
\item \textbf{No stochastic loss}: All experiments use lossless links.
\item \textbf{Simulator only}: No Mahimahi or real-network validation.

\end{enumerate}





\section{Conclusion}

We compared two additional representations under Remy's training and
evaluation setup---a neural network (PPO) and an LLM-guided code
evolution system (AlphaCC)---alongside Remy's decision trees. For the
main comparison, Remy and PPO were trained on a single condition
(15~Mbps, 150~ms RTT, 2~senders); AlphaCC was evaluated in both
single-point and 6-rate multipoint configurations. All methods
were evaluated across 1--1000~Mbps. Three findings:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Representation shapes generalization.} Each
  representation degrades differently outside training. Remy peaks
  near training but degrades sharply at both extremes. AlphaCC
  degrades more gradually at high rates. No method dominates
  everywhere (Figure~\ref{fig:cpp_gen}).

\item \textbf{Mechanistic convergence.} Across 12~independent
  AlphaCC runs from 4~seed families, every evolved policy contains
  in-flight scaling and rate-based pacing (12/12).
  Whether this reflects the LLM's TCP prior or a fitness-landscape
  attractor remains an open question (\S\ref{sec:convergence}).

\item \textbf{Reliable mechanism discovery $\neq$ reliable
  performance.} AlphaCC's evolution is high-variance (means
  $-$1.40 to $-$3.53 across 12~runs), and seed family choice
  strongly affects outcome (pacing median $-$1.79 vs.\ AIMD
  $-$3.05).
\end{enumerate}
